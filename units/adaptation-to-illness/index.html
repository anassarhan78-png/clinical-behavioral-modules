Adaptation to Illness – Breaking Bad News (SPIKES)

📌 Slide 1: Adaptation to Illness – Overview
Original English Text:
This week focuses on how illness affects quality of life, and on adapting to illness. A patient with a serious medical illness, such as cancer, faces multiple challenges. Key questions: How does this patient adapt? How does she rate her quality of life after the news? What factors determine quality of life? What coping strategies are needed? What social support is required?
الترجمة إلى العربية:
يركز هذا الأسبوع على كيفية تأثير المرض على جودة الحياة، والتكيف مع المرض. المريض الذي يعاني من مرض خطير مثل السرطان يواجه تحديات متعددة. الأسئلة الرئيسية: كيف يتكيف هذا المريض؟ كيف يقيم جودة حياته بعد الخبر؟ ما العوامل التي تحدد جودة الحياة؟ ما استراتيجيات التأقلم المطلوبة؟ ما الدعم الاجتماعي اللازم؟
🩺 Slide 2: Clinical Case – Mrs. Sonia (Breast Cancer)
Original English Text:
Name: Mrs. Sonia. Age: 29. Married with two children. Address: Al-hasa. Profession: Teaching. General Information: The patient is healthy and does not take any medication. No family history of serious illness. Main health issues: Having a headache, fever for several days. The pain increases under exertion. Found a lump in the breast after self-examination a couple of months ago. She was worried & anxious; too busy. She comes to GP for advice expecting to be reassured. Diagnosis: Breast cancer.
Breaking Bad News (SPIKES Model):
Setting up: Eye contact. Perception: Checking misinterpretation. Invitation: "Would you like me to give the information about the test results?" Knowledge: "Sorry, today I am going to tell you that..." Empathy: Care and support. Summary & Strategy: Treatment plan: Surgery, chemotherapy, radiation. Share decision making. Second stage cancer, 90% cure rate, 5-10% recurrence, follow-up every 6 months. Referral to Oncologist.
الترجمة إلى العربية:
الاسم: السيدة سونيا. العمر: 29. متزوجة ولديها طفلان. العنوان: الهفوف. المهنة: معلمة. المعلومات العامة: المريضة سليمة ولا تتناول أي أدوية. لا يوجد تاريخ عائلي لأمراض خطيرة. الأعراض الرئيسية: صداع، حمى، ألم يزداد مع الجهد. وجدت كتلة في الثدي بعد الفحص الذاتي قبل عدة أشهر. كانت قلقة ومتوترة ومشغولة جداً. تأتي إلى طبيب الأسرة للحصول على نصيحة وتتوقع الطمأنة. التشخيص: سرطان الثدي.

نموذج سبايكس لإخبار الخبر السيئ:
تهيئة المكان: تواصل بصري. الإدراك: التحقق من سوء الفهم. الدعوة: "هل ترغبين في معرفة نتائج الفحوصات؟" المعرفة: "أنا آسف، اليوم سأخبرك أن..." التعاطف: رعاية ودعم. الملخص والاستراتيجية: خطة العلاج: جراحة، علاج كيميائي، إشعاعي. اتخاذ القرار المشترك. سرطان المرحلة الثانية، نسبة الشفاء 90٪، نسبة عودة 5-10٪، متابعة كل 6 أشهر. تحويل إلى أخصائي الأورام.
🌍 Slide 3: Quality of Life (WHO Definition)
Original English Text:
According to the WHO Quality of Life working group (1993,1994), QoL is a person's perceptions of their position in life in relation to their cultural context and the value systems of that context in relation to their own goals, standards and expectations.
Many factors influence QoL: demographics (age, culture); the condition itself (symptoms, pain, disability, emotional or cognitive impairment); treatment (curable, duration, severity, side-effects); psychosocial factors (emotions like anxiety/depression, lack of social support, lack of guidance).
الترجمة إلى العربية:
وفقاً لفريق عمل منظمة الصحة العالمية المعني بجودة الحياة (1993، 1994)، فإن جودة الحياة هي تصورات الشخص لمكانته في الحياة فيما يتعلق بسياقه الثقافي وأنظمة القيم في ذلك السياق فيما يتعلق بأهدافه ومعاييره وتوقعاته.
هناك العديد من العوامل التي تؤثر على جودة الحياة: التركيبة السكانية (العمر، الثقافة)؛ الحالة نفسها (الأعراض، الألم، الإعاقة، الضعف العاطفي أو المعرفي)؛ العلاج (قابل للشفاء، المدة، الشدة، الآثار الجانبية)؛ العوامل النفسية الاجتماعية (مشاعر مثل القلق والاكتئاب، نقص الدعم الاجتماعي، نقص التوجيه).
😔 Slide 4: Common Emotional Responses in Cancer Patients
Original English Text:
Cancer patients often get frustrated. They may experience: Denial (not believing); Anger (why myself? anger to family, spouse, God); Fear (frustration, anxiety, helpless); Worry (pain of cancer treatment, cost, keeping the job); Loneliness (aloof, distance from friends, peers, relatives); Physical disorder (heartbeat, high blood pressure, headache, muscle pain, weak, dizziness, diarrhea); Worry about recurrence.
الترجمة إلى العربية:
غالباً ما يشعر مرضى السرطان بالإحباط. قد يعانون من: الإنكار (عدم التصديق)؛ الغضب (لماذا أنا؟ غضب على الأسرة، الزوج، الله)؛ الخوف (الإحباط، القلق، العجز)؛ القلق (ألم العلاج الكيميائي، التكلفة، الحفاظ على الوظيفة)؛ الوحدة (الانعزال، المسافة عن الأصدقاء والأقارب)؛ الاضطراب الجسدي (خفقان القلب، ارتفاع ضغط الدم، الصداع، آلام العضلات، الضعف، الدوخة، الإسهال)؛ القلق من عودة المرض.
💪 Slide 5: Ways to Cope with Emotion (Positive Strategies)
Original English Text:
Look for positive way: Hope and encouragement. Do not blame yourself for illness: Controlling and be patient. Find ways to relax: Managing yourself, peace of mind e.g., yoga, exercise, meditation. Be active and search for alternative: How to deal and come out from that stress life. Positive thinking. Hope: Positive motivation. Hobbies: Reading any books or others which keep you busy.
الترجمة إلى العربية:
ابحث عن طريقة إيجابية: الأمل والتشجيع. لا تلوم نفسك على المرض: التحكم والصبر. ابحث عن طرق للاسترخاء: إدارة نفسك، راحة البال مثل اليوجا، التمارين، التأمل. كن نشطاً وابحث عن بدائل: كيفية التعامل والخروج من حياة التوتر. التفكير الإيجابي. الأمل: الدافع الإيجابي. الهوايات: قراءة أي كتب أو أي شيء يبقيك مشغولاً.

📝 30 Essay Questions with Model Answers (Click to expand)

Each question includes a detailed model answer, explanation of why the answer is correct, and common mistakes.

1️⃣ Explain the SPIKES model step by step using the case of Mrs. Sonia. Why is each step crucial when breaking bad news?
✅ Correct Answer: SPIKES: Setting (private, eye contact) – builds trust. Perception (ask what patient already knows) – avoids repetition and shock. Invitation (ask permission to share results) – respects autonomy. Knowledge (give medical facts clearly) – avoids confusion. Empathy (acknowledge emotions) – reduces psychological distress. Summary/Strategy (treatment plan, next steps) – provides hope and direction. ❌ Common mistake: Skipping permission and empathy leads to increased anxiety and poor recall of information.
2️⃣ Define Quality of Life according to WHO. List at least 5 factors that influence QoL in a cancer patient like Mrs. Sonia.
✅ Definition: "Perceptions of position in life in relation to cultural context and value systems, goals, standards, expectations." Factors: 1) Age and culture, 2) Physical symptoms (pain, fatigue), 3) Treatment side-effects (nausea, hair loss), 4) Emotional state (anxiety, depression), 5) Social support (family, friends). ❌ Common mistake: Only focusing on physical symptoms and ignoring psychological and social dimensions.
3️⃣ Describe the common emotional reactions of cancer patients as mentioned in the slide. How can a physician recognize and manage each?
✅ Reactions: Denial (patient says "it can't be true") – allow time, gentle reinforcement. Anger ("why me?") – listen without judgment, validate feelings. Fear (trembling, crying) – provide information, reassurance. Worry (about cost, job loss) – refer to social worker. Loneliness – encourage support groups. Physical symptoms (insomnia, palpitations) – treat symptomatically and consider psychiatry referral. ❌ Common mistake: Dismissing anger as "bad attitude" rather than a normal grief response.
4️⃣ What is the difference between denial and avoidance? Give clinical examples from breast cancer patients.
✅ Denial: Active rejection of reality (e.g., patient says "the biopsy is wrong, I don't have cancer"). ✅ Avoidance: Evading situations that remind of the illness (e.g., not coming to follow-up appointments). Both are defense mechanisms. Management: denial may require gentle confrontation; avoidance requires reducing perceived threat. ❌ Common mistake: Forcing acceptance too quickly – can worsen anxiety.
5️⃣ How does the cultural background of a patient (e.g., Middle Eastern, Indian) affect adaptation to cancer? Apply to Mrs. Sonia (age 29, married, teacher).
In collectivist cultures, family plays a central role. Mrs. Sonia may worry about stigma, being a burden, and ability to care for her children. She may also fear being seen as "weak". The physician should involve family in discussions (with patient permission) and respect religious beliefs. Provide culturally sensitive psychoeducation. ❌ Mistake: Assuming Western individualistic coping strategies work universally.
6️⃣ Explain the concept of "coping strategies". Differentiate between emotion-focused and problem-focused coping. Give examples for Mrs. Sonia.
Coping strategies are conscious efforts to manage stress. Emotion-focused: managing feelings (e.g., relaxation, meditation, talking to a friend). Problem-focused: taking action to change the situation (e.g., scheduling chemotherapy, seeking second opinion). Mrs. Sonia needs both: emotion-focused for anxiety about death; problem-focused for treatment decisions. ❌ Mistake: Only using emotion-focused coping without practical planning leads to helplessness.
7️⃣ What is the role of a social support network for a 29-year-old mother with breast cancer? Name at least 6 types of supporters.
Types: partner (emotional and childcare), family members (parents, siblings), friends (social outings, listening), co-workers (understanding work absences), neighbors (practical help), community members (support groups). Each provides different functions. Lack of support increases depression and treatment dropout. ❌ Mistake: Relying solely on partner can lead to caregiver burnout.
8️⃣ Describe the SPIKES step "Invitation". Why is it essential to ask "Would you like me to give you the test results?" rather than just stating them?
Invitation respects patient autonomy. Some patients may not want full details (especially in some cultures where family prefers to receive bad news first). Asking permission reduces shock and gives the patient a sense of control. If patient refuses, the physician can ask "Who would you like me to share the results with?" ❌ Mistake: Assuming all patients want full disclosure immediately.
9️⃣ How can a physician demonstrate empathy during breaking bad news? Provide specific phrases and non-verbal behaviors.
Non-verbal: sitting down, maintaining gentle eye contact, leaning forward, offering tissues. Verbal: "I can see this is very difficult news." "I'm sorry to have to tell you this." "Many people feel overwhelmed at first – we will take this step by step." "I will be here to support you." ❌ Mistake: Saying "I know how you feel" – which can feel dismissive. Instead, validate unique experience.
🔟 What are the potential long-term psychological consequences of poorly delivered bad news? How can SPIKES prevent them?
Poor delivery can cause long-term distress: PTSD symptoms, mistrust of doctors, treatment refusal, chronic anxiety, depression. SPIKES prevents these by: setting (privacy reduces shame), perception (corrects misinformation), empathy (prevents feeling abandoned), summary (gives clear plan). ❌ Mistake: Rushing through news to "get it over with" causes lasting harm.
1️⃣1️⃣ Compare the grieving process in cancer patients (Kübler-Ross model) with adaptation to illness. Where might Mrs. Sonia be?
Kübler-Ross stages: Denial, Anger, Bargaining, Depression, Acceptance. Mrs. Sonia may be in shock (denial) initially, then anger. Adaptation is not linear; patients oscillate. Physician must meet patient at current stage. ❌ Mistake: Expecting acceptance too soon.
1️⃣2️⃣ What is the difference between "cure rate" and "recurrence rate"? Explain 90% cure and 10% recurrence to Mrs. Sonia in simple terms.
Cure rate = probability of being disease-free after treatment. Recurrence rate = probability cancer returns. Explain: "90% means 9 out of 10 women with your stage are cured. 10% chance it might come back – that's why we do regular checks." ❌ Mistake: Stating percentages without context causes fear.
1️⃣3️⃣ Describe the role of a psychologist in the oncology team. How would Dr. Nurjahan Begum help Mrs. Sonia?
Psychologist assesses emotional distress, provides cognitive behavioral therapy, teaches relaxation techniques, addresses fear of recurrence, and supports family members. Dr. Begum would help Sonia reframe catastrophic thoughts and build coping skills. ❌ Mistake: Referring only when patient is "very depressed" – early intervention is better.
1️⃣4️⃣ What is "post-traumatic growth" in cancer survivors? Can Mrs. Sonia achieve it? Give examples.
Post-traumatic growth: positive psychological changes after adversity – e.g., stronger relationships, appreciation of life, new priorities. Yes, with support, Sonia may become more resilient, closer to family, and advocate for breast cancer awareness. ❌ Mistake: Assuming all survivors will grow – some experience chronic distress.
1️⃣5️⃣ How would you explain to Mrs. Sonia the need for a 6-month follow-up when she feels "cured"? Use motivational interviewing.
"I understand you want to put this behind you. At the same time, follow-ups help us catch any small change early, so you stay healthy. What are your thoughts about coming back every 6 months?" ❌ Mistake: Scaring her with "if you don't come, cancer will come back".
1️⃣6️⃣ Discuss the concept of "shared decision making" in breaking bad news. How can the doctor involve Mrs. Sonia in treatment choices?
After explaining options (surgery, chemo, radiation), ask: "What matters most to you? Are you concerned about fertility, time off work, side effects?" Offer choices and respect her values. This increases adherence and satisfaction. ❌ Mistake: Paternalistically deciding "best treatment" without patient input.
1️⃣7️⃣ What is the impact of a cancer diagnosis on a patient's professional life, especially for a teacher like Mrs. Sonia? How to support her?
She may need time off for treatment, fear of losing job, or reduced performance due to fatigue. Support: request medical leave, flexible hours, talk to school administration, involve occupational health. ❌ Mistake: Advising her to quit work without exploring accommodations.
1️⃣8️⃣ Explain the term "cancer-related fatigue". How is it different from normal tiredness? What non-pharmacological interventions help?
Cancer-related fatigue is overwhelming, not relieved by rest, often due to treatment or inflammation. Interventions: gentle exercise, energy conservation, cognitive behavioral therapy, nutrition support. ❌ Mistake: Telling patient "just rest more" – may worsen deconditioning.
1️⃣9️⃣ How would you address Mrs. Sonia's worry about "being a burden" to her husband and children?
Normalize this concern. Explore: "What makes you feel like a burden?" Then reframe: "Many people feel this way, but your family may feel grateful to help." Encourage open family discussion and delegate tasks. ❌ Mistake: Dismissing as "don't worry" without validation.
2️⃣0️⃣ Compare the prognosis of stage 2 breast cancer with stage 4. Why is stage 2 considered curable?
Stage 2: localized or limited regional spread, high cure rate (80-95%) with standard treatment. Stage 4: metastatic, not curable but treatable. The key difference: surgery and radiation can remove all cancer in stage 2. ❌ Mistake: Equating all cancers as similarly deadly.
2️⃣1️⃣ What is an "oncologist"? Describe the role of a medical oncologist vs. a radiation oncologist in breast cancer care.
Medical oncologist: manages chemotherapy, hormonal therapy, targeted therapy. Radiation oncologist: delivers radiation therapy. Both work together with a surgeon. Mrs. Sonia will likely see all three. ❌ Mistake: Assuming one doctor handles everything.
2️⃣2️⃣ How can a physician detect "maladaptive coping" in a cancer patient? Give signs and appropriate intervention.
Signs: social withdrawal, substance abuse, non-adherence to treatment, persistent severe anxiety, suicidal ideation. Intervention: psychiatric referral, cognitive behavioral therapy, or medication. ❌ Mistake: Waiting until crisis develops.
2️⃣3️⃣ What is the "nocebo effect" in cancer treatment? How can it affect Mrs. Sonia's experience?
Nocebo effect: negative expectations cause worse side effects. If Mrs. Sonia expects severe nausea from chemo, she may experience it more intensely. Discuss realistic side effects and positive coping. ❌ Mistake: Overemphasizing rare severe side effects.
2️⃣4️⃣ Describe the importance of "summarizing" in the SPIKES model. What should the summary include?
Summary reinforces key information, checks understanding, and outlines next steps. Include: diagnosis, treatment plan, prognosis, follow-up schedule, who to contact for questions. Ask patient to repeat back. ❌ Mistake: Assuming patient remembers everything after emotional shock.
2️⃣5️⃣ How would you help Mrs. Sonia talk to her young children about her cancer? Provide age-appropriate language.
For young children: "Mommy has an illness called cancer. The doctors are giving me medicine to help me get better. You didn't cause it, and you can't catch it." Reassure them of continued love and care. ❌ Mistake: Hiding the truth can increase children's anxiety when they sense something is wrong.
2️⃣6️⃣ What is "survivorship care plan"? Why is it important for Mrs. Sonia after completing treatment?
A survivorship care plan summarizes treatment received, potential late effects, follow-up schedule, and healthy lifestyle recommendations. It empowers the patient and coordinates care with primary care physician. ❌ Mistake: Discharging patient without any plan leads to lost follow-up.
2️⃣7️⃣ Discuss the ethical principle of "beneficence" versus "non-maleficence" when breaking bad news.
Beneficence: providing truthful information to allow informed decisions. Non-maleficence: avoiding harm by being sensitive, not causing unnecessary distress. SPIKES balances both: truth with empathy. ❌ Mistake: Withholding truth to "protect" patient (paternalism) violates autonomy and beneficence.
2️⃣8️⃣ How can a physician assess the patient's perception before breaking bad news? Provide sample questions.
Ask: "What have you been told so far about your tests?" "What do you understand about the lump you found?" "Have you had any thoughts about what this might be?" This clarifies misconceptions and gauges readiness. ❌ Mistake: Assuming the patient knows nothing or already knows everything.
2️⃣9️⃣ Explain the term "palliative care". Is it only for end-of-life? Why might Mrs. Sonia benefit early?
Palliative care is specialized care for symptom management and quality of life, appropriate at any stage, alongside curative treatment. Early palliative care reduces anxiety, depression, and even improves survival in some cancers. Mrs. Sonia can benefit from pain and nausea management. ❌ Mistake: Believing palliative care means "giving up".
3️⃣0️⃣ Summarize the key psychological interventions that can improve adaptation to breast cancer. Provide a stepwise plan for Mrs. Sonia's first month after diagnosis.
Week 1: Validate emotions, provide SPIKES-based information, refer to psychologist. Week 2: Psychoeducation on side effects, relaxation training. Week 3: Introduce support group, involve family. Week 4: Cognitive restructuring for catastrophic thoughts, set realistic goals. Long-term: regular follow-up, promote physical activity, monitor for depression. ❌ Mistake: Focusing only on physical treatment and ignoring psychological needs.
